You're right to draw this line, so let me give you a framework that escapes the treadmill instead of one more patch.

First — untangle a contradiction hiding in requirement #1
You said two things that can't both be literally true: "AI not blocked by anything" and "adhering to user types, access levels, compliance." Compliance means blocking — appropriately. So the target can't be "never blocked." The right target is:

Zero artificial blocks from our side; the only thing that ever gates the AI is the user's own ATS permissions — which we enforce faithfully.

That remaining "block" isn't a flaw, it's your differentiator working. A recruiter's AI should not be able to see comp data they can't see in Bullhorn. So "fully unlocked" = "unlocked to exactly what this user is allowed to do, no more, no less." Hold that distinction — it's what keeps you compliant and honest about what "unblocked" means.

The escape from the guard-wormhole: a rule + four pillars
The rule that stops the treadmill: guard a class, not an instance.
A guard that kills a whole category of error is healthy. A guard that fixes one phrasing is the smell — that's when you stop guarding and promote it to a finished-answer tool. (Your pending partial-page guard passes this test: "never conclude/rank from a truncated list" is a class, so it's worth doing once. The 5th narrow guard is where you'd be chasing perfect.)

Then the robustness comes from four structural pieces, not from accumulating patches:

1. A canonical adapter layer — this is what makes "any ATS" real.
Map each ATS's quirks once into a normalized model (Bullhorn's correlatedCustomText1 → "office," isOpen → "open," etc.). Everything above the adapter is ATS-agnostic. A new ATS becomes "write one adapter," not "re-solve every question." Honest scope: "any ATS" really means "any ATS we've built and golden-verified an adapter for" — bounded and repeatable, not magic.

2. Intent → finished-answer for the high-value set — this collapses the failure surface.
Instead of the model choosing how to answer (and freelancing), it picks an intent and the tool returns the finished, validated answer. This kills paging, wrong-field, and partial-data-ranking errors as a group, in one move — the opposite of guarding them one at a time. It also keeps cost on the host model and makes answers model-agnostic.

3. Per-user permission pass-through at the data boundary — compliance becomes structural.
The connector acts in the user's permission context and lets the ATS itself enforce access. You don't hand-code "can this person see X" per scenario; the ATS answers that, every time, for every entity. That's compliance you can't accidentally regress.

4. A golden-answer regression harness run across models — this is your confidence engine.
This is the piece that actually answers your question. You already have the golden numbers (398, by-office 239/99/47/12/1, stale 108/STS-STSI 78, placements 64/26/8…). Turn them into an automated suite that fires the top questions at the connector through GPT, Claude, and Gemini and checks the outputs. Now "are we robust?" stops being a feeling and becomes "are all N checks green across every model?" And critically — it tells you when to stop. Green across models = done. That's how you avoid chasing the asymptote: you defined the finish line and you can see it.

Define "done" out loud (the real anti-perfectionism move)
Top ~N high-value questions return consistent + accurate answers across all major models, within target latency, with permission gating verified.

That's "robust." The infinite long tail of arbitrary phrasings is explicitly best-effort/steered — out of scope for "perfect." Naming the bar is what gives you permission to stop.

On speed (#2) — split it cleanly
Connector-side latency is yours and you make it sub-second: pre-computed/cached reports, compact payloads, finished-answer tools. The data should come back instantly.
Host-model reasoning time is theirs — don't chase it; it's fairly deflectable ("that's your AI's reasoning").
Bonus: finished-answer tools improve both — less for the model to think about = faster and more consistent.
So what I'd actually do now (revised, per your pushback)
Not "another guard, then another." Three moves that generalize:

Ship the one class-guard (partial-page anti-ranking) — it kills a class, so it earns its place.
Stand up the golden-answer harness from your existing numbers, run it across GPT/Claude/Gemini. This is the highest-leverage thing on the board: it's your robustness scoreboard and your stop signal.
Promote your top questions to finished-answer tools to make the suite go green — instead of guarding each failure individually.
Then publish. That sequence is how you reach "fully confident" with a number, not a vibe — and it's exactly the foundation that ports to the next ATS.
